What Is an LBE and Why Does It Matter for San Francisco Small Businesses?

By Rosales Law Partners LLP • June 2026 • 5 min read

If your small business is based in San Francisco, LBE certification could be one of the most immediately practical steps you take to grow. The City of San Francisco spends billions of dollars annually on contracts for goods and services — and LBE certified businesses have a built-in advantage when competing for them.

What Is an LBE?

LBE stands for Local Business Enterprise. It is a certification issued by the San Francisco Office of Contract Administration (OCA) for businesses that are principally located in, or that make a significant commitment to, San Francisco.

The LBE program is governed by Chapter 14B of the San Francisco Administrative Code. It is designed to ensure that a meaningful portion of the City’s contracting dollars stay within the local economy and benefit San Francisco businesses and workers.

What Are the Benefits of LBE Certification?

LBE certification provides concrete advantages in City contracting:

  • Bid preference: LBE certified businesses receive a percentage preference in the scoring of competitive bids, making your proposal more competitive against non-certified businesses.
  • Set-aside contracts: Many City contracts are reserved specifically for LBE certified businesses, particularly contracts below certain dollar thresholds.
  • Subcontracting goals: Prime contractors on large City projects are required to use LBE subcontractors for a portion of the work — creating ongoing opportunities even when you are not bidding as a prime.
  • Networking and visibility: LBE certification puts your business in the City’s procurement database, making it easier for prime contractors and agencies to find you.

Who Qualifies for LBE Certification?

To qualify as an LBE, your business generally must meet the following criteria:

  • Location: The business must be principally located in San Francisco — meaning your primary place of business, where employees regularly work, is within San Francisco city limits.
  • Ownership and control: The business must be owned and controlled by individuals who meet LBE criteria. There are several LBE subcategories with different ownership requirements (see below).
  • Size: The business must meet size standards based on annual gross receipts or number of employees, depending on the industry.
  • Independent operation: The business must be independently owned and operated, not dominated by a larger non-certified business.

LBE Subcategories

There are several LBE subcategories that may provide additional preferences:

  • MBE (Minority Business Enterprise): At least 51% owned by one or more minority individuals.
  • WBE (Women Business Enterprise): At least 51% owned by one or more women.
  • SBE (Small Business Enterprise): Meets size standards as a small business.
  • SLBE (Small and Local Business Enterprise): Meets both LBE and SBE criteria — and typically receives the highest bid preference.
  • ELBE (Emerging Local Business Enterprise): A newer, smaller business with limited revenues — designed to help businesses at the earliest stage compete.

How to Apply for LBE Certification

LBE certification applications are submitted to the San Francisco Office of Contract Administration. The process generally involves:

  • Completing the OCA certification application form
  • Providing business formation documents (articles of incorporation, operating agreement, etc.)
  • Proving principal location in San Francisco (lease agreements, utility bills, etc.)
  • Providing ownership documentation and personal financial information
  • Submitting recent tax returns

The OCA may conduct a site visit and interview as part of the process. Certification decisions are typically made within 60 days of a complete submission.

Can I Hold Both LBE and DBE Certification?

Yes — and many small businesses in San Francisco do. LBE applies to City of San Francisco contracts, while DBE applies to federally funded transportation contracts. The two certifications are independent of each other and serve different markets. Holding both maximizes the number of contracting opportunities your business can access. We often recommend that qualifying businesses pursue both certifications simultaneously.

What Happens After Certification?

Once certified, your business will appear in the City’s LBE directory, which is used by City departments and prime contractors when searching for certified subcontractors. You will need to renew your certification annually and notify the OCA of any significant changes to your business.

Active participation in City contracting — attending pre-bid conferences, networking with prime contractors, and monitoring the City’s procurement portal (SF City Buy) — is essential to turning certification into real contract wins.
