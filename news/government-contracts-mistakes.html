5 Legal Mistakes Small Businesses Make When Bidding on Government Contracts

By Rosales Law Partners LLP • June 2026 • 6 min read

Winning a government contract can be transformative for a small business. But the bidding process is heavily regulated — and small errors can disqualify your proposal entirely. After more than 17 years helping small businesses compete for public contracts, we have seen the same mistakes come up again and again.

Here are the five most common legal mistakes — and how to avoid them.

Mistake #1: Submitting an Incomplete or Non-Compliant Proposal

Government RFPs (Requests for Proposals) include detailed instructions about exactly what must be submitted, in what format, by what deadline. Agencies are not obligated to give you a chance to fix a non-compliant bid — and many will disqualify it outright.

Common compliance failures include:

  • Missing required certifications or attachments
  • Submitting in the wrong format (e.g., PDF when Word was required)
  • Exceeding page limits
  • Failing to acknowledge all addenda to the RFP
  • Missing the deadline by even a few minutes

How to avoid it: Create a compliance checklist from the RFP before you write a single word. Have an attorney or experienced colleague review your bid against that checklist before submission.

Mistake #2: Not Understanding the Scope of Work

Many small businesses submit bids without fully understanding what they are agreeing to do. Government contracts can have hidden obligations — reporting requirements, insurance minimums, prevailing wage laws, bonding requirements, and more — that significantly affect your costs and risk.

Bidding too low because you missed these obligations is a common way to win a contract and lose money on it.

How to avoid it: Read the full contract draft, not just the scope of work. Have an attorney flag terms that could create unexpected liability or cost. Ask questions through the official RFP question period — agencies are required to answer, and the answers become part of the solicitation.

Mistake #3: Not Registering in SAM.gov Before Bidding

To bid on any federal government contract, your business must be registered in SAM.gov (System for Award Management). Registration takes time to process, and an expired or inactive registration will get your bid rejected — no matter how strong your proposal is.

How to avoid it: Register in SAM.gov now, before you identify a specific contract. Renew your registration annually. It is free to register directly through SAM.gov — be aware of third-party services that charge fees for this.

Mistake #4: Misrepresenting DBE or SBE Participation

Government contracts often require that a percentage of the work go to certified DBE or SBE firms. Some prime contractors misrepresent DBE participation — listing certified firms in their bid but not intending to actually use them, or understating the percentage they plan to use.

For small businesses on the receiving end, this is an important issue: if you are listed as a DBE subcontractor in a bid without your knowledge or consent, you may have legal recourse. And if you are the prime contractor, misrepresenting DBE participation can result in bid disqualification, contract termination, and federal debarment.

How to avoid it: Only list DBE/SBE firms you have genuinely committed to use. Get written documentation of every subcontractor commitment before the bid is submitted. If you are a small business being listed, ask for a copy of the relevant contract language.

Mistake #5: Missing the Protest Deadline When You Lose

If you believe a contract was awarded improperly — because the winning bidder did not meet requirements, the agency made a scoring error, or the process was not followed correctly — you have the right to file a bid protest. But bid protest deadlines are extremely short: typically 10 days from the date the award is announced at the federal level, with varying deadlines at state and local agencies.

Many small businesses discover these rights too late, after the deadline has passed.

How to avoid it: Monitor award announcements actively. If something seems wrong, contact a procurement attorney immediately — do not wait to gather information first.

The Bottom Line

Government contracting is a significant opportunity for small businesses — but the process is unforgiving of errors. The good news is that most of these mistakes are entirely preventable with proper preparation and legal review. A small investment in getting it right before submission can be the difference between winning and being disqualified.
